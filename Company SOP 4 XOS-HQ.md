# XOS SOP

## Timestamp
20260410 205917 EDT

## Purpose
This SOP defines the system-wide operating procedure for XOS.

It governs shared operating rules that apply across all divisions, departments, offices, teams, positions, worker agents, and tools.

It sits below the Constitution and Articles and above all lower SOP layers.

## 1. Scope and Authority

### 1.1 Scope
This SOP applies to all XOS operating layers unless a higher governing document states otherwise.

### 1.2 Authority order
When information conflicts, use this authority order unless a higher document states otherwise:

1. Constitution and Articles
2. XOS SOP
3. Division SOP
4. Department SOP
5. Office SOP
6. Team SOP
7. Position SOP
8. AGENT.md
9. approved repo-level operating documents
10. durable operating state and approved memory files
11. compressed or retrieved historical material
12. live conversation context

### 1.3 Lower-layer rule
Lower SOPs inherit this SOP.

### 1.4 Specialization rule
Lower SOPs may narrow procedures for their own scope, but may not silently replace or weaken this SOP.

## 2. Authority Flow Procedure

### 2.1 Human authority
The Human Executive is the final authority for:

1. canon
2. governance
3. priorities
4. structural direction
5. cross-division conflict resolution
6. irreversible or high-impact decisions
7. final approval of material spend and scope expansion

### 2.2 Downward delegation
Work, direction, and authority flow downward through the structure:

1. Human Executive
2. Executive-level agents
3. Division heads
4. Department heads
5. Office or team leads
6. Worker agents
7. Micro-agents or tools

### 2.3 Upward reporting
Status, blockers, risks, conflicts, decisions, and spend exceptions flow upward.

### 2.4 Upward reporting triggers
A layer must report upward when:

1. a blocker exceeds local authority
2. a canon conflict appears
3. timing risk becomes material
4. a dependency threatens execution
5. a decision requires higher approval
6. cross-scope ownership becomes unclear
7. spend exceeds approved context
8. scope expands materially

## 3. Canon and Source Handling Procedure

### 3.1 Conflict handling
If two sources materially conflict, the handling owner must:

1. name the conflict explicitly
2. identify the sources involved
3. identify the apparent higher-authority source
4. identify what remains unresolved
5. escalate if local authority is insufficient

### 3.2 Source preservation
Imported or reference material must be preserved before reinterpretation, merge, rewrite, promotion, or canonization.

### 3.3 Assumption separation
Assumptions must be kept separate from canon, evidence, and confirmed fact whenever interpretation or execution materially depends on them.

## 4. Logging Procedure

### 4.1 Minimum logging duty
All agents must log, at minimum, when relevant:

1. task starts
2. task completions
3. important decisions
4. blockers
5. escalations
6. major outputs
7. dependency changes
8. approval decisions
9. spend-related exceptions

### 4.2 Durable record rule
Conversation alone is not considered durable recordkeeping for important operational decisions.

If something matters beyond the moment, it must be written to an approved file or system of record.

### 4.3 Logging timing
Logging should occur:

1. at task start when the task is material
2. when state changes materially
3. when a blocker appears or clears
4. when a decision changes future work
5. when a deliverable becomes durable
6. when approval or denial changes next action

## 5. Time Awareness Procedure

### 5.1 Minimum time awareness
All agents must maintain awareness of:

1. current date
2. current time
3. deadlines
4. due conditions
5. checkpoints
6. elapsed work time when work is active
7. dependency timing when relevant

### 5.2 Time handling rule
Agents must not treat time as implied when timing affects execution.

If timing matters, it must be made explicit in state, logging, scheduling, or communication.

### 5.3 Due-state handling
Agents must distinguish between:

1. not yet due
2. due
3. at risk
4. overdue

## 6. Escalation Procedure

### 6.1 Escalation triggers
Escalation is required when:

1. authority is unclear
2. canon is conflicting or absent
3. the next step is destructive
4. the next step is public
5. the next step creates financial risk
6. the next step is security-sensitive
7. the next step is materially irreversible
8. a dependency delay now threatens execution
9. a cross-division conflict appears
10. risk exceeds local authority
11. approved spend context is about to be exceeded

### 6.2 Escalation content
An escalation must state:

1. what happened
2. what is blocked or at risk
3. what sources or parties are involved
4. what authority boundary was hit
5. what decision, asset, permission, or approval is needed next

## 7. Handoff Procedure

### 7.1 Required handoff elements
A valid handoff must identify:

1. current owner
2. receiving owner
3. objective
4. required inputs
5. expected output
6. due condition or timing expectation
7. escalation path if blocked

### 7.2 Handoff rule
No handoff may leave ownership ambiguous.

Until ownership is accepted or reassigned, the original owner remains responsible for follow-through.

## 8. Task State Procedure

### 8.1 Minimum task states
All XOS work should be understandable in terms of at least these states:

1. not started
2. active
3. blocked
4. deferred
5. complete

### 8.2 State change rule
Material task-state changes must be updated promptly enough to preserve execution clarity.

## 9. Durable Output Procedure

### 9.1 Durable output rule
If work needs to persist, be reviewed, be reused, guide future action, or support accountability, it must become a durable output.

### 9.2 Durable output examples
Durable outputs include:

1. decisions
2. specifications
3. SOP updates
4. architecture notes
5. approved plans
6. review artifacts
7. structured summaries
8. state files that govern ongoing work
9. approved proposals
10. budget approvals and exceptions

### 9.3 Live-state rule
Live state belongs in approved operating-state files or systems, not inside durable governing files unless the live state itself is the governed object.

## 10. Review Procedure

### 10.1 Review trigger
Material work should be reviewed before being treated as settled.

### 10.2 Minimum review questions
Review should ask:

1. Is the output structurally clear?
2. Are assumptions separated from settled facts?
3. Are dependencies identified?
4. Are risks named?
5. Is ownership clear?
6. Does the output reduce or increase future friction?

## 11. Budgeting, Proposal, and Spend Authorization Procedure

### 11.1 Core rule
No head agent may initiate or expand meaningful spend without an approved budget context.

### 11.2 Approved budget contexts
Approved budget context must exist in one of these forms:

1. Standing Operating Budget
2. Project Proposal Budget
3. Exception Request

### 11.3 Standing Operating Budget
A Standing Operating Budget covers recurring, expected, ongoing work inside a normal operating period.

### 11.4 Standing Operating Budget minimum contents
A Standing Operating Budget must include:

1. operating objective
2. covered activities
3. normal staffing pattern
4. normal tool and model usage
5. expected monthly spend band
6. approved monthly ceiling
7. escalation threshold for overage

### 11.5 Project Proposal Budget
A Project Proposal Budget is required for bounded work with a defined objective, deliverables, and end state.

### 11.6 Project Proposal triggers
A Project Proposal Budget is required when:

1. the work is new and not covered by a standing budget
2. the work introduces a new tool, provider, model lane, or paid dependency
3. the work materially expands worker count, routing complexity, or execution scope
4. the work has distinct deliverables or a defined completion package
5. the work creates meaningful spend outside normal operations

### 11.7 Project Proposal minimum contents
Every project proposal must include:

1. objective
2. scope in
3. scope out
4. assumptions
5. deliverables
6. dependencies
7. staffing plan
8. model-routing plan
9. tool shopping list
10. skill shopping list
11. risks
12. stop conditions
13. escalation triggers
14. budget range
15. maximum spend ceiling
16. what finished looks like

### 11.8 Budget bands
Every project proposal must include these three budget bands:

1. best case
2. expected
3. ceiling

### 11.9 Exception Request
An Exception Request is required for any work that exceeds an approved standing budget or approved project ceiling.

### 11.10 Exception Request minimum contents
An Exception Request must include:

1. what changed
2. why the original budget no longer holds
3. revised forecast
4. new ceiling requested
5. consequence of approval
6. consequence of denial
7. recommended decision

### 11.11 Accountability rule
Budget preparation may be delegated downward.

Budget accountability may not be delegated downward.

The supervising head remains responsible for completeness, accuracy, escalation, and spend discipline.

## 12. Project Definition and Completion Procedure

### 12.1 Project definition rule
No project proposal is complete unless scope and success are explicitly defined.

### 12.2 Scope definition
Scope must identify:

1. what is included
2. what is excluded
3. what decisions are already locked
4. what remains unsettled

### 12.3 Success definition
Success must be defined as a finished state, not activity.

### 12.4 What finished looks like
What finished looks like must describe the concrete done-state in operational terms.

### 12.5 Acceptable completion examples
Acceptable completion examples include:

1. schema finalized
2. tests passing
3. routing policy defined
4. budget controls configured
5. retrieval quality validated
6. migration path documented
7. handoff package ready
8. risks documented and accepted

### 12.6 Unacceptable completion examples
Unacceptable completion examples include:

1. made progress
2. first pass done
3. mostly complete
4. ready for next phase without explicit criteria

## 13. Bulletin and Announcement Procedure

### 13.1 Bulletin classes
Bulletins must be classified as either:

1. Governance Bulletin
2. Informational Bulletin

### 13.2 Governance Bulletin use
Governance Bulletins are used for:

1. SOP updates
2. directives
3. approvals
4. security notices
5. canon locks
6. spend-rule changes

### 13.3 Informational Bulletin use
Informational Bulletins are used for:

1. announcements
2. reminders
3. general notices
4. non-governing updates

### 13.4 Bulletin rule
A bulletin announces or surfaces a rule, update, or notice.

A bulletin does not replace the governing SOP or approval record.

### 13.5 Bulletin scope
Bulletins must be scoped to the lowest audience that still preserves awareness.

## 14. Inheritance and Specialization Procedure

### 14.1 Layer split
Use this split consistently:

1. XOS SOP = system-wide procedure
2. Division SOP = division-wide procedure
3. Department SOP = department-wide procedure
4. Position SOP = seat-specific procedure

### 14.2 Downstream specificity rule
The higher the SOP layer, the broader and more governing it should be.

The lower the SOP layer, the more custom, particular, and procedural it should become.

### 14.3 Duplication rule
Lower documents should specialize downward instead of repeating large sections of XOS SOP unless repetition is necessary for operational clarity.
